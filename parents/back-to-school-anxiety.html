Helping a Kid Through Back-to-School Anxiety

Five evidence-based things parents can do when a kid is anxious about a new school year — and one thing to avoid that most well-meaning parents do anyway.

The week before school starts is one of the most common times for a child to fall apart. Stomach aches. Insomnia. Refusal to talk about school, or to talk about anything else. Sudden tears at bedtime. If you're a parent reading this in early August, none of it is unusual. It's also not a sign you've done something wrong.

Anxiety about a transition is the brain doing its job. School is new (or in the case of a returning year, freshly uncertain). The brain runs through scenarios. Some of those scenarios involve things going badly. The body reacts to imagined scenarios with the same chemistry it would use for real ones, which is why a worry can feel physical.

What helps is straightforward to list and harder to execute. Here's what the literature on childhood anxiety actually supports.

1. Name what's happening — out loud, with words

Anxiety gets bigger when it's vague. The first move is to help the child put words around the feeling. Not "you're fine" (which dismisses it) and not "tell me what's wrong" (which often produces tears without information).

Try: "Your body's working really hard right now. I think part of you is worried about school starting. Does that sound right?"

You're not solving anything yet. You're labeling. Naming an emotion engages the parts of the brain that can think about it instead of just feeling it.

2. Let the worries be ridiculous

When a child finally tells you what they're worried about, the contents are often weird. "What if my teacher has a weird voice." "What if no one sits with me at lunch." "What if I forget my locker combination and have to live at school forever."

The temptation is to fix each worry with logic. Don't. The worry isn't the actual problem — the underlying uncertainty is. Fixing one worry just produces three new ones.

Instead, listen, repeat back, and resist the urge to immediately reassure. "You're worried about lunch. That makes sense. Lunch is one of the parts of the day you can't really plan for." That's it. The child doesn't need an action plan. They need to be heard.

3. Reduce specific uncertainties where you can

Anxiety responds to known information the way fire responds to oxygen-deprivation: it gets smaller when there's less fuel. So where you can reduce uncertainty without manufacturing false comfort, do it.

  • Walk the school route together a few days before classes start, if possible.
  • Identify which kids from last year will be in the same grade.
  • If there's a teacher meet-and-greet, attend it.
  • Pack the backpack together the night before, not the morning of.

These are small. The point isn't that any one of them solves the anxiety. The point is that several small known things crowd out the unknown.

4. Keep your own anxiety off-screen

This one is hard. If you're anxious about your child being anxious, the child reads it instantly. They watch your face when they tell you about school. If your face tightens, they update their mental model: "school is the kind of thing that makes Mom tighten her face."

You don't have to fake being relaxed. You can have your own feelings — just process them when the child isn't in the room. Text a friend. Talk to your partner after bedtime. Don't make the child the regulator of your nervous system.

5. Schedule the worry, then close the topic

Older kids (8 and up) often benefit from a designated "worry time" — a 15-minute slot, usually right after dinner, where they can dump anything they're anxious about. The rest of the day, the rule is: if it's not in worry time, it can wait until worry time.

This sounds clinical and it works surprisingly well. The brain doesn't need worries to be solved. It needs to know they'll be acknowledged. Worry time gives it that guarantee, and the rest of the day quiets down.

The one thing to avoid

Don't promise the anxiety will go away.

It usually does. But not always. And you don't actually know on day one whether this particular school year will go smoothly or whether it'll involve a hard period that requires a counselor. If you promise "you'll be fine" and the child has a rough first week, you've damaged the trust around this topic, and they'll be less likely to come to you next time.

Instead: "I don't know how the first week will go. I'm here either way. We'll figure it out together."

When to call someone

If the anxiety is persistent (more than three weeks past the start of school), is interfering with sleep most nights, or includes refusal to attend — talk to the pediatrician. They can refer you to a child psychologist or therapist who specializes in anxiety. The earlier you bring in support, the easier it is.
