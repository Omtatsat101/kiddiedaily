What is media bias?
🤔

Framing bias

The same fact can sound different depending on the words chosen. "Police responded to a protest" vs. "Police cracked down on demonstrators" — same event, different frame.

🔍

Selection bias

News outlets choose WHAT to cover. A story that's big on Fox News might barely appear on NPR — and vice versa. Neither absence means it didn't happen.

📢

Tone bias

Word choice, headline intensity, and photo selection all carry emotion. Positive tone toward one group, negative toward another — that's bias at work.

⚖️

Source bias

Who gets quoted? When one side of a story gets more expert voices than the other, the story feels more credible even if the evidence isn't.

How to fact-check any story — 5 steps
1

Find the original claim. What exactly is being said? A headline often exaggerates. Read past the first paragraph before reacting.

2

Who is the source? Is it a named expert, an anonymous source, or "studies show"? Named, on-record sources are more reliable than unnamed ones.

3

Search for the same story elsewhere. Does the BBC report it the same way as Fox News? If the facts differ, someone may have gotten it wrong — or be spinning it.

4

Check a fact-checking site. Google Fact Check Explorer, Snopes, and PolitiFact all search thousands of verified claims. Type any claim into their search bar.

5

Ask: what is this story missing? Good reporting names who benefits, who is harmed, what happened before, and what experts disagree. Missing any of these? Keep reading.

💡 Family activity: spot the framing

Pick any story from today's KiddieDaily. Then:

  • Read the KiddieDaily version
  • Search the headline on Google News and find 2 other outlets covering it
  • Ask: What words did each outlet choose? What did they emphasize? What did they leave out?
  • Discuss: Is the core fact the same across all three? What's different?

This is how journalists learn to read news critically — and it works for kids as young as 9.
