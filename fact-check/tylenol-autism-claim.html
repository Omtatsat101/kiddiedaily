False / Misleading

Claim: "Tylenol causes autism"

A claim that has been repeated in political speeches and viral social media posts: that acetaminophen (Tylenol, paracetamol) use during pregnancy causes autism in children. The strongest available evidence does not support a causal link.

What was claimed

In late 2024 and recurring through 2025-2026, public figures including the U.S. president have stated, in interviews and rallies, that Tylenol use during pregnancy causes autism. The specific phrasing varies but typically asserts a direct causal relationship.

What the research actually shows

The strongest single study addressing this question is a 2024 sibling-control analysis published in JAMA by Ahlqvist et al. It used Sweden's national medical registers to compare siblings born to the same mother — some exposed to prenatal acetaminophen, others not. The sibling-control design controls for genetic and family-environment factors that confounded earlier observational studies.

The result: when comparing siblings, prenatal acetaminophen exposure was not associated with autism, ADHD, or intellectual disability. Earlier studies that found small associations had not controlled for genetic and shared-environment confounders. When those confounders are controlled for, the apparent association disappears.

The American Academy of Pediatrics issued a statement following the JAMA study reaffirming that acetaminophen, used as directed, remains the safest available over-the-counter pain reliever and fever reducer for pregnant patients. The Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists similarly maintained their guidance.

What the earlier studies showed

Several observational studies prior to 2024 reported small associations between prenatal acetaminophen exposure and neurodevelopmental outcomes. These studies have important limitations: they couldn't separate the effect of the medication from the effect of the underlying condition the medication was treating (fever, pain). Fever in pregnancy itself has documented developmental effects.

A 2021 consensus statement signed by some researchers called for "precautionary action" based on these observational findings. That statement was controversial within the field, and the 2024 sibling-control study materially changed the evidence base.

Why this matters

Acetaminophen is one of the very few pain and fever medications considered safe in pregnancy. Most alternatives (NSAIDs like ibuprofen, aspirin) carry known risks at various stages. If pregnant patients avoid acetaminophen based on a false belief about autism causation, untreated fever — which has its own documented risks — becomes more likely.

The claim "Tylenol causes autism" is not supported by the strongest available evidence and may cause direct harm by leading pregnant patients to refuse a safe medication when they need it.

What pregnant patients should do

Follow guidance from your obstetrician. The current major-society guidance from ACOG, AAP, and SMFM continues to recommend acetaminophen for pain or fever in pregnancy at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration needed, as has been the case for decades.

Primary sources

  • Ahlqvist et al., "Acetaminophen Use During Pregnancy and Children's Risk of Autism, ADHD, and Intellectual Disability," JAMA, 2024 — sibling-control analysis using Swedish registers
  • American Academy of Pediatrics statement on prenatal acetaminophen, September 2024
  • American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists position statement, 2024
  • Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine consult series, 2024
  • Bauer et al., "Paracetamol use during pregnancy — a call for precautionary action," Nature Reviews Endocrinology, 2021 — the earlier consensus statement, since contested

How to read the studies yourself

If you want to verify our reading: search PubMed for "acetaminophen pregnancy autism sibling control" for the most recent peer-reviewed evidence. The JAMA paper is the most important single citation.
