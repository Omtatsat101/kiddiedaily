A Family in India Grew Vegetables on the Roof — and Started a Whole Movement

A grandmother in Bangalore turned her concrete rooftop into a vegetable garden. Five years later, her neighborhood has 200 rooftop farms.

Bangalore is a big, busy city in southern India. Like most big cities, it doesn't have much room for farms. Most food has to be trucked in from far away, which costs money and uses a lot of fuel.

Mrs. Saraswathi, a grandmother in a Bangalore neighborhood, decided to try something different. Six years ago, she filled some old plastic containers with soil and started growing tomatoes, spinach, and chilies on her rooftop.

At first her neighbors thought it was strange. Now they all do it.

How it works

You don't need a lot of space. A few large containers, some good soil, and seeds will start you off. Sunlight on a rooftop is usually plentiful, and rain helps with watering. The tricky part is making sure the roof can hold the weight when the containers are wet.

Mrs. Saraswathi's neighbors started with one container each. Some now have a dozen. One family grows almost all their vegetables on their roof.

Why it adds up

Rooftop gardening in cities does three useful things at once:

  • It cools the building below by adding plants on top, which means lower air-conditioning bills.
  • It cuts the distance food has to travel from farm to table down to a flight of stairs.
  • It teaches kids where food comes from.

None of these is huge by itself. But when 200 households in a neighborhood do it, the effect adds up.

The math

The neighborhood association estimates that the 200 rooftop farms together produce about 30,000 kilograms of vegetables a year. That's enough to feed roughly 50 families for a year, just from soil that used to be empty concrete.

The bigger lesson

Mrs. Saraswathi is now 74 and still tends her garden every morning. She told a local reporter something nice: "I didn't try to start a movement. I just wanted my granddaughter to know what a tomato plant looked like before it turned into a tomato."

Sometimes the things that change the most are the ones that started small.
