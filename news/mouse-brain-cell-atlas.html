Scientists Just Mapped Every Cell in a Mouse Brain

A team of researchers spent four years counting and identifying every single cell in a mouse brain. They found there are far more types of brain cells than anyone had guessed.

The brain is the most complicated thing in your body. It has about 86 billion cells called neurons, and each one connects to thousands of others. For a long time, scientists guessed there were maybe a few hundred different types of brain cells. Now they have a real number, at least for mice.

The number: more than 5,000 different cell types.

That's not 5,000 individual cells. That's 5,000 different kinds of cells, like how a forest has different kinds of trees but each kind has many individual trees in it.

How did they count them?

The team, working at the Allen Institute in Seattle, used a technique called single-cell RNA sequencing. Every cell in your body has the same DNA, but different kinds of cells "read" different parts of it. By looking at what each cell is reading, scientists can tell what kind of cell it is.

They did this for over 11 million brain cells from mice. That's about the population of Belgium, but in cells, and from one small animal's brain.

Why this matters

Most diseases that affect the brain — Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, autism, depression, ALS — affect specific kinds of cells more than others. If doctors know which kind of cell is going wrong, they can design medicines that target just those cells without messing up the others.

It's like having a map. Before this study, brain scientists had a sketch. Now they have something closer to a satellite image.

What's next

The same team is now mapping a human brain. That's going to take a lot longer because humans have about 1,000 times more brain cells than mice. But the technique works, and they're well on the way.

One of the lead scientists said something interesting in the press conference: "We thought we'd find fewer types than we did. The brain is more complicated than we thought, even when we thought we were being generous."

Try this at home

Next time you do something tricky — like learning a new word or remembering where you left your shoes — remember that 5,000 different kinds of cells are working together inside your head to make that happen. It's a lot of cells. And a lot of types.
