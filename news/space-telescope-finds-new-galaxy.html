Scientists Find a Surprising New Galaxy

The James Webb Space Telescope sent back a picture of a galaxy that's making scientists look at the universe a little differently.

The James Webb Space Telescope is the biggest, most powerful telescope humans have ever launched into space. It's been floating about a million miles from Earth for the last few years, sending back pictures of things that are very far away and very old.

This week, scientists got a picture from Webb of a galaxy nicknamed JADES-GS-z14-0. (Don't worry about the name — even astronomers think it's a mouthful.) What's interesting about this galaxy isn't the name. It's the age.

Why this galaxy is special

This galaxy is about 13.4 billion years old. That means we're seeing it as it looked just 290 million years after the Big Bang — basically the universe's baby pictures.

Here's the surprise: galaxies that young aren't supposed to be this big. Most theories say it should take much longer for stars to clump together into galaxies this large. But Webb keeps finding ones that broke the rules.

What this means

It probably doesn't mean our theories are wrong. It might mean galaxies formed faster in the early universe than we thought — or that we were undercounting the dust and gas around them. Scientists are doing the math and will publish their best guess in a few months.

The bigger lesson: every time we build a better telescope, we find things we didn't expect. That's how science works. You ask a question, you get an answer, and the answer gives you three new questions.

Try this at home

If you have clear skies tonight, go outside and look up. You can see the Andromeda galaxy with your eyes alone if you know where to look — it's the closest big galaxy to ours, and the light from it has been traveling for 2.5 million years to reach your eyeball. That light started moving when our earliest human ancestors were just learning to make stone tools.
